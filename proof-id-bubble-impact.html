Characterization: When IDs Can Affect Bubble Statistics

Author: Analysis of Swiss Tournament Simulator
Date: 2024
Question: Under what conditions on cut size (C) and number of rounds (R) can intentional draws affect bubble statistics?

1. Definitions and Setup

Definitions:

  • C: Cut size (number of players who advance, e.g., C = 4 for Top 4, C = 8 for Top 8)
  • R: Total number of rounds in the tournament
  • r: Remaining rounds when ID decision is made (r ∈ {1, 2} since IDs only occur in last 2 rounds)
  • N_passers(S): Number of players who could potentially finish with score ≥ S
  • Safety margin: How many additional potential passers a player can tolerate and still be safe

2. The Safety Condition

A player with current score S is safe after drawing if:

Npassers(S + 1) < C

That is, fewer than C players can potentially finish with score ≥ S + 1.

A player is safe after losing if:

Npassers(S) < C

That is, fewer than C players can potentially finish with score ≥ S.

3. When Can IDs Affect Bubbles?

Characterization: IDs can affect bubble statistics if and only if there exists a scenario where:
  1. Two players A and B are both safe if they draw: Npassers(SA + 1) < C and Npassers(SB + 1) < C
  2. But if A loses (getting 0 instead of 1): Npassers(SA) ≥ C

This happens when the safety margin is exactly 0 or 1 for at least one player.

4. Constraints on Safety Margin

4.1 Maximum Possible Score Gains

When an ID decision is made with r remaining rounds:

  • Any other player (not in the match) can gain at most 3r points
  • The opponent in the match can gain at most 1 + 3r points (if they draw) or 3 + 3r points (if they win)

4.2 Players Who Can Cross the S → S+1 Threshold

For IDs to affect bubbles, we need players who can reach exactly S but not S + 1.

A player q with current score Sq can reach S but not S + 1 if:

Sq + 3r ≥ S and Sq + 3r < S + 1

Which simplifies to: S - 3r ≤ Sq < S - 3r + 1

This is only possible if S ≡ 0 \pmod{3} or S ≡ 1 \pmod{3} (since scores are multiples of 3, with possible +1 for draws).

5. Characterization by Cut Size and Rounds

Key Insight: Safety Margin Distribution

The safety margin for a player with score S is:

margin(S) = C - 1 - Npassers(S + 1)

For IDs to NOT affect bubbles, all players who are safe must have:

margin(S) ≥ 1

This means they can tolerate at least one additional potential passer and still be safe.

5.1 Small Cut Sizes (C ≤ 4)

Observation: With small cut sizes, the safety margin is naturally small.

Example with C = 4, R = 5, in round 4 (r = 1):

  • If player has score S = 12 and N_passers(13) = 2, then margin = 4 - 1 - 2 = 1
  • If N_passers(12) = 3, then losing would give N_passers(12) = 3 ≥ 4? No, still safe
  • But if N_passers(12) = 4, then losing gives N_passers(12) = 4 ≥ 4, NOT safe!

Conclusion: With small cuts, players are more likely to be at the safety boundary.

5.2 Large Cut Sizes (C ≥ 8)

Observation: With large cut sizes, there's more "room" in the safety margin.

Example with C = 8, R = 5, in round 4 (r = 1):

  • If player has score S = 12 and N_passers(13) = 6, then margin = 8 - 1 - 6 = 1
  • Even if N_passers(12) = 7, losing still gives margin ≥ 0, so still safe
  • Need N_passers(12) = 8 to become unsafe, which requires more players in similar score range

Conclusion: With larger cuts, players need to be closer to more competitors to be at the boundary.

5.3 Number of Rounds

Observation: More rounds means more point spread, fewer players in similar ranges.

With R = 3 rounds:

  • Maximum score spread: 0 to 9 points
  • Many players can be in similar score ranges
  • Higher probability of players being at safety boundary

With R = 7 rounds:

  • Maximum score spread: 0 to 21 points
  • Players are more spread out
  • Fewer players in similar score ranges near the cut

Conclusion: Fewer rounds → more clustering → higher chance IDs affect bubbles.

6. Formal Characterization

Theorem 1: Necessary Condition for IDs to NOT Affect Bubbles

If IDs cannot affect bubble statistics, then for all players A, B who are safe to draw:

Npassers(SA + 1) ≤ C - 2 or equivalently Npassers(SA) ≤ C - 1

Proof: If IDs cannot affect bubbles, then losing instead of drawing cannot change safety status. Therefore:

  • Npassers(SA + 1) < C (safe after draw)
  • Npassers(SA) < C (safe after loss)

Since Npassers(SA) ≥ Npassers(SA + 1), we need at least a margin of 1, giving the condition above.

Theorem 2: Sufficient Condition (Sufficiently Large Safety Margins)

If for all tournament states where IDs occur, all safe players have:

Npassers(S + 1) ≤ C - 2

Then IDs cannot affect bubble statistics.

Proof: If Npassers(S + 1) ≤ C - 2, then:

Npassers(S) ≤ Npassers(S + 1) + k

where k is the number of players who can reach S but not S + 1.

The maximum k is bounded by the tournament structure, but even in worst case:

Npassers(S) ≤ C - 2 + k < C (for sufficiently separated players)

Therefore, losing instead of drawing maintains safety.

7. Practical Characterization

When IDs are UNLIKELY to Affect Bubbles:

  1. Large cut size (C ≥ 8): More "room" in the cut means players are safer, larger safety margins
  2. Many rounds (R ≥ 6): Greater point spread means fewer players clustered near cut line
  3. Early tournament stage: But IDs only occur in last 2 rounds, so this is fixed
  4. High draw chance: More unintentional draws reduce the number of ID opportunities

When IDs are MORE LIKELY to Affect Bubbles:

  1. Small cut size (C ≤ 4): Tight cut means players closer to boundary
  2. Few rounds (R ≤ 4): Less point spread means more players in similar ranges
  3. Low draw chance: More deterministic outcomes create clearer score clusters
  4. Large player pools: More players increases chance of having exactly C-1 or C players in a score range

8. Mathematical Relationship

The key relationship is:

IDs affect bubbles ⇔ ∃ players A, B such that:

  • Npassers(SA + 1) = C - 1 (exactly at boundary)
  • Npassers(SA) ≥ C (would cross boundary if lose)

This requires: There exist at least C players (excluding A, B) who can reach score SA.

9. Empirical Observations

Cut Size (C) Rounds (R) Likelihood IDs Affect Bubbles Reason
4 3-4 High Small cut + few rounds → tight clusters
4 5-6 Medium Small cut but more spread
4 7+ Low More spread reduces clustering
8 3-4 Medium More room in cut, but still tight
8 5-6 Low Good balance: enough spread, enough room
8 7+ Very Low Large cut + lots of spread
16 Any Very Low Very large cut size creates large safety margins

10. Conclusion

Summary: IDs can affect bubble statistics when players are "barely safe" (at the exact safety threshold). This is more likely to occur with:

  • Small cut sizes (C ≤ 4) - creates tighter boundaries
  • Few rounds (R ≤ 4) - creates score clustering
  • Large player pools - increases probability of having exactly C players in a score range

IDs are unlikely to affect bubbles when:

  • Large cut sizes (C ≥ 8) - larger safety margins
  • Many rounds (R ≥ 6) - greater point spread, less clustering
  • Safety margins ≥ 1 for all players who would ID

Key Insight: The relationship is not a simple function of C and R alone, but depends on the distribution of player scores near the cut line. However, smaller cuts and fewer rounds increase the probability of score clustering, which increases the likelihood that IDs can affect bubble statistics.
